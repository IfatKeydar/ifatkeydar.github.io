Research focus

The Electric Genome Hypothesis

The spatial organization of the nucleus shows remarkable regularity, yet its underlying physical mechanism remains unresolved. The Electric Genome Hypothesis proposes that a steady electric field at the nuclear envelope acts on charged macromolecules, so that the nucleus effectively functions as a spherical electrophoresis apparatus.

Although DNA is uniformly negative, the effective charge of chromatin is not. Bound proteins such as histones, transcription factors, and other DNA associated proteins modify local charge. Their binding patterns are influenced by sequence features, for example higher nucleosome density in GC rich regions. This generates an uneven charge landscape that shapes how chromatin and nuclear bodies respond to the nuclear field.

In this framework, GC rich, less negative chromatin and positively charged bodies like speckles and nucleoli tend to occupy internal positions, while more negative, AT rich chromatin remains peripheral. Charge dependent positioning provides a physical route to allele specific expression bias and variable disease penetrance, by turning subtle sequence or epigenetic differences between homologs into spatial and functional asymmetry.

Key ingredients

  • Radial electric field maintained at the nuclear envelope.
  • Uneven chromatin charge set by protein binding and sequence context.
  • Reproducible radial positioning as an electrostatic equilibrium.
  • Integration with loop extrusion, phase separation, and nuclear bodies.

Background

About

I am an independent scientist based in Tel Aviv, working at the interface of genomics, nuclear organization, and biophysics. My research interests focus on how physical principles, particularly electrostatics, can explain the reproducible yet flexible organization of the genome in three dimensions.

My previous work includes studies of allele specific expression in the human brain and a Molecular Cell paper linking nuclear positioning to splicing regulation. These lines of thought eventually converged into the Electric Genome Hypothesis, which proposes that chromatin charge and electrostatic forces provide a unifying mechanism for nuclear architecture and expression bias.

I am especially interested in collaborations with experimental groups who work on nuclear imaging, genome organization, and chromatin biophysics, and who may wish to test or refine aspects of this model.

Research style

I tend to work from concept to mechanism, starting from simple physical principles and then asking what patterns they can explain in existing data.

  • Preference for testable and falsifiable models.
  • Integration of physics, genomics, and nuclear imaging.
  • Attention to radial organization, allele asymmetry, and disease penetrance.

Location

Tel Aviv, Israel. Working independently, with existing and potential collaborations in Europe and North America.
